Who Do You Call Savior?

Chapter Four

The Love That Sent a Savior

The Love That Sent a Savior

Throughout the previous chapters, we have discovered who Jesus Christ is, why every person stands equally in need of Him, and how sin has separated humanity from God. These truths lead us to an important question. If God is perfectly holy and humanity has rebelled against Him, why would He choose to save us at all? Why would the Creator pursue those who had rejected His authority? Why would He provide a way of redemption when justice alone would have been deserved?

The answer is found in the love of God.

The love of God is unlike any love that we experience in this world. Human love is often influenced by emotions, circumstances, and personal benefit. It can grow stronger or weaker depending upon how people are treated. God's love, however, flows from His unchanging character. He does not love because humanity deserves His affection. He loves because love is part of who He is. His love is holy, faithful, perfect, and everlasting.

From the opening pages of Scripture, God revealed His desire to have fellowship with the people He created. Even after sin entered the world and humanity rebelled against Him, God's purpose did not change. Rather than abandoning His creation, He began revealing His plan to redeem it. Every covenant, every promise, and every prophecy pointed toward the day when His love would be fully displayed through Jesus Christ.

Many people think of God's love as merely an emotion or a feeling of compassion. The Bible presents something much greater. God's love is demonstrated through action. It is seen in His patience toward sinners, His mercy toward the undeserving, His faithfulness to His promises, and ultimately His willingness to provide the sacrifice that humanity could never provide for itself.

This truth reaches its greatest expression in one of the best known verses in all of Scripture. Although it is frequently quoted, its significance should never become ordinary. These words summarize the heart of the gospel and reveal the motivation behind God's plan of redemption.

"For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life."
John 3:16

Notice that the verse does not begin with humanity's love for God. It begins with God's love for humanity. Before we ever sought Him, He was already working to accomplish our redemption. Before we understood our need, He had already prepared the answer. Salvation did not begin in the heart of man. It began in the heart of God.

The love of God is not merely expressed through words. Throughout the Scriptures, His love is consistently demonstrated through His actions. From the moment humanity fell into sin, God began revealing His plan to restore what had been broken. Generation after generation, He remained faithful to His promises, even when people repeatedly turned away from Him. His patience, mercy, and long suffering testify to a love that does not abandon those whom He created.

When we read the Old Testament, we discover that the coming of Jesus Christ was never an afterthought. God was not reacting to humanity's failure as though He had been caught by surprise. Before the foundation of the world, He already knew the cost of redemption and had determined to accomplish His purpose through the Savior who would come into the world. Every prophecy, every covenant, and every sacrifice pointed toward that day.

The prophets repeatedly spoke of the Messiah long before His birth. Isaiah described Him as the suffering Servant who would bear the sins of many. Micah foretold His birthplace in Bethlehem. David wrote of His suffering centuries before crucifixion became a method of execution. Zechariah spoke of His betrayal, and countless other passages reveal details that were fulfilled in the life and ministry of Jesus Christ. These prophecies remind us that God's plan of redemption unfolded exactly as He intended.

The birth of Jesus was therefore much more than the arrival of an extraordinary teacher. It was the fulfillment of God's promise to send the Redeemer who would reconcile humanity to Himself. In Christ, God's love entered human history in a visible and personal way. The eternal Word became flesh and dwelt among us, revealing both the holiness of God and His compassion toward sinners.

"But God commendeth his love toward us, in that, while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us."
Romans 5:8

This verse reveals one of the most remarkable truths in all of Scripture. God's love was not dependent upon humanity first becoming righteous. Christ did not die for people who had already earned God's favor or proven themselves worthy of His acceptance. He died while we were still sinners. His sacrifice was not a response to our goodness but an expression of His grace.

Human love often depends upon the actions of others. It is easy to love those who love us in return or to show kindness toward those who have treated us well. God's love is altogether different. He loved humanity while we were separated from Him by sin. He extended mercy to those who deserved judgment and offered reconciliation to those who had rebelled against His authority. Such love cannot be explained by human reasoning alone. It finds its source in the perfect character of God Himself.

Understanding God's love also helps us understand the purpose of the cross. Jesus Christ did not come into the world because God was forced to act. He came because God willingly chose to rescue those who could never rescue themselves. Every step that Jesus took toward Calvary revealed the immeasurable depth of the Father's love and His determination to provide salvation for all who would believe.

The love of God never stands in opposition to His holiness. Throughout the Scriptures, these two attributes work together in perfect harmony. God is infinitely loving, yet He is also perfectly righteous. He is rich in mercy, yet He never compromises His justice. To understand the cross correctly, we must recognize that God's love did not ignore sin. Rather, His love provided the means by which sin could be justly forgiven.

If God simply overlooked sin without addressing its penalty, He would no longer be perfectly just. Likewise, if He dealt only in justice without extending mercy, humanity would have no hope of redemption. The gospel reveals the wisdom of God in accomplishing both. At the cross, His justice was satisfied, and His mercy was freely extended. What appeared to many as an instrument of suffering became the greatest demonstration of God's love ever revealed.

Jesus Christ willingly accepted the mission that the Father had given Him. He did not come as an unwilling victim, nor was His death the result of circumstances beyond His control. From the very beginning of His earthly ministry, Jesus understood why He had come into the world. Every miracle He performed, every sermon He preached, and every step He took brought Him closer to the cross that He had willingly embraced for the salvation of mankind.

"Greater love hath no man than this, that a man lay down his life for his friends."
John 15:13

These words were spoken by Jesus shortly before His crucifixion. He was preparing His disciples for what was about to take place. Although they did not yet understand everything that would happen, Jesus knew that He would soon lay down His life willingly. His sacrifice was not motivated by obligation but by love. The cross was the ultimate expression of His obedience to the Father's will and His compassion for those He came to save.

Throughout history, countless individuals have sacrificed themselves for others. Soldiers have given their lives to protect their country. Parents have willingly endured hardship for the sake of their children. Friends have risked everything to rescue those they love. These acts deserve honor and respect, yet the sacrifice of Jesus Christ stands alone. No other person has ever borne the sins of the entire world or provided redemption through the offering of a perfectly sinless life.

The uniqueness of Christ's sacrifice is found not only in what He did but also in who He is. Because He lived without sin, He alone was qualified to become the perfect sacrifice for sinners. Every Old Testament sacrifice pointed toward this moment. Every lamb placed upon an altar anticipated the day when the true Lamb of God would take away the sin of the world. What generations had awaited was finally fulfilled in Jesus Christ.

When we consider the cross, we should never see it merely as an historical event. It is the place where God's eternal plan of redemption was accomplished. There, the debt that humanity could never repay was fully satisfied through the sacrifice of Jesus Christ. His death was sufficient because His life was perfect. His sacrifice was acceptable because He alone was without sin.

The cross therefore reveals both the seriousness of sin and the immeasurable greatness of God's love. If there had been another way to reconcile humanity to God, the cross would not have been necessary. The fact that Jesus willingly endured suffering and death reminds us that our redemption required nothing less than the perfect sacrifice that only He could provide.

The story of redemption does not end with the crucifixion. If Jesus Christ had remained in the tomb, the hope of salvation would have ended there as well. The cross alone demonstrates the depth of God's love, but the resurrection declares the triumph of that love. On the third day, Jesus Christ rose from the dead, fulfilling the Scriptures and proving that sin and death had been conquered forever.

The resurrection is not an isolated event added to the gospel account. It is central to the Christian faith. The apostles did not merely proclaim that Jesus had died for sinners. They boldly declared that He had risen again and was alive forevermore. The empty tomb became the foundation of their preaching and the source of their unwavering confidence. They understood that the resurrection confirmed everything Jesus had taught about Himself and everything the prophets had foretold concerning the coming Messiah.

"But now is Christ risen from the dead, and become the firstfruits of them that slept."
1 Corinthians 15:20

Because Jesus Christ lives, the promise of eternal life is no longer merely a hope for the future. It is a certainty grounded in the finished work of God. The resurrection demonstrates that death was not victorious, the grave could not hold the Savior, and the sacrifice offered upon the cross was fully accepted by the Father. Every promise of forgiveness, reconciliation, and everlasting life rests upon the reality that Jesus Christ is alive.

The resurrection also assures believers that their faith is not founded upon legend or philosophy but upon the living Christ. Christianity is unique because its message centers upon a risen Savior who continues to reign. The disciples did not devote their lives to preserving the memory of a deceased teacher. They proclaimed the living Lord whom they had seen with their own eyes after His resurrection. Their testimony transformed the world because it was rooted in historical reality and confirmed by the power of God.

The love of God is therefore seen not only in sending His Son to die but also in raising Him again in victory. Through the resurrection, God declared that sin had been defeated, the power of death had been broken, and the way into His presence had been opened for all who believe. The empty tomb stands as an enduring testimony that God's plan of redemption was accomplished exactly as He had promised.

Every chapter of Scripture points toward this glorious reality. The promises made in the Garden of Eden, the sacrifices offered under the Law, the words spoken by the prophets, and the ministry of Jesus Christ all find their fulfillment in His death, burial, and resurrection. The love of God is not an abstract idea or a passing emotion. It is a historical reality demonstrated through the greatest act of redemption the world has ever known.

When we understand the love that sent the Savior, we begin to understand the heart of the gospel itself. God's desire has always been to reconcile sinful humanity to Himself. He did not wait for us to find our way back to Him. He came to us through Jesus Christ, making a way where no way existed. His love reached farther than our sin, His mercy extended beyond our failures, and His grace accomplished what we could never accomplish for ourselves.

"For God sent not his Son into the world to condemn the world; but that the world through him might be saved."
John 3:17

The mission of Jesus Christ was never one of condemnation but of salvation. He came to seek and to save those who were lost, to reconcile sinners to God, and to offer the gift of eternal life to all who would believe. His life, death, and resurrection reveal the greatest expression of divine love ever made known to humanity.

The Greatest Demonstration of Love

The Cross Was God's Plan

God's love was not demonstrated by words alone but by the willing sacrifice of Jesus Christ. In the next chapter, we will look more closely at the cross itself and discover that it was not a tragic accident of history but the fulfillment of God's eternal plan of redemption.
