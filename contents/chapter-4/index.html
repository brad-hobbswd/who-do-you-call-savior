Who Do You Call Savior?

Chapter Four

When Grace Finds the Broken

When Grace Finds the Broken

Every person carries a story. Some stories are filled with joy, while others are marked by disappointment, regret, failure, loss, addiction, loneliness, or broken relationships. Some wounds are visible for the world to see, while others remain hidden deep within the heart. Although our experiences differ, every person shares one common reality. We all live in a world that has been affected by sin, and every one of us stands in need of the grace that only Jesus Christ can provide.

Grace is God's undeserved favor given to those who could never earn it.

The message of the gospel is remarkable because it begins where human hope often ends. Jesus did not come into the world searching for people who believed they had everything together. He came seeking those who knew they were lost. He did not reserve His invitation for the wealthy, the educated, the influential, or the religious elite. He extended His invitation to ordinary people whose lives had been shaped by failure, weakness, disappointment, and sin.

Throughout His earthly ministry, Jesus continually demonstrated that the kingdom of God was open to those whom society frequently overlooked. Fishermen left their nets to become disciples. Tax collectors found forgiveness instead of condemnation. Men and women whose lives had been shattered by disease, grief, and hopelessness discovered that the Savior welcomed them with compassion. Wherever Jesus traveled, broken lives were transformed by His presence.

The religious leaders of His day often struggled to understand this. They expected the Messiah to spend His time among those who appeared outwardly righteous. Instead, Jesus repeatedly welcomed those whom others considered unworthy. He sat with tax collectors, spoke with outcasts, touched lepers, forgave sinners, and restored those whom society had rejected. His actions revealed the very heart of God.

"They that are whole have no need of the physician; but they that are sick: I came not to call the righteous, but sinners to repentance."
Mark 2:17

Jesus was not suggesting that some people were without sin. Rather, He was exposing the difference between those who recognized their need and those who believed they had none. A physician is sought by those who know they are sick. In the same way, the Savior is welcomed by those who recognize their need for His grace. The gospel begins with humility, acknowledging that apart from Jesus Christ, no one can save themselves.

This truth remains just as important today. Every person who comes to Christ comes the same way. No one earns a place before Him through personal achievement, religious tradition, family heritage, or moral success. We come because we need His mercy. We come because His grace is greater than our failures. We come because He alone has the power to forgive, restore, and make all things new.

The grace of Jesus Christ was never limited by a person's past. Throughout the Gospels, we repeatedly see Him looking beyond outward appearances and seeing the heart. While others judged people by their failures, social standing, or reputation, Jesus saw individuals created in the image of God who needed redemption. His compassion reached those whom others ignored, and His mercy extended to those who believed they had no hope.

One of the clearest examples of this truth is found in His encounter with the Samaritan woman at Jacob's well. By every cultural standard of the day, she was someone others would have avoided. She was a Samaritan, a people despised by many Jews. She was a woman drawing water alone during the hottest part of the day, likely because she had become an outcast within her own community. Her personal life had been marked by broken relationships and disappointment. Yet Jesus intentionally traveled through Samaria because He knew she would be there.

Rather than condemning her, Jesus began a conversation that would forever change her life. He acknowledged the reality of her past without allowing her past to define her future. He offered her something no earthly well could ever provide. He offered her living water that would satisfy the deepest thirst of the human soul.

"But whosoever drinketh of the water that I shall give him shall never thirst."
John 4:14

The woman came to the well carrying the weight of her past, but she left with the hope of a new beginning. Grace had found her. The woman who once avoided the people of her city returned to tell them about the Savior she had met. Her testimony became one of the first public declarations that Jesus was truly the Christ.

Grace also found a man named Zacchaeus. As a chief tax collector, he had become wealthy through a profession that was despised by many of his fellow Jews. To countless people, Zacchaeus represented dishonesty, greed, and betrayal. Few would have expected Jesus to notice him, much less choose to spend time in his home.

When Jesus entered Jericho, Zacchaeus climbed into a sycamore tree simply to catch a glimpse of Him. Instead of passing by, Jesus stopped, looked up, called him by name, and invited Himself into Zacchaeus's house. That invitation surprised everyone who witnessed it, because they expected Jesus to avoid someone with such a reputation.

"For the Son of man is come to seek and to save that which was lost."
Luke 19:10

The encounter transformed Zacchaeus. His response demonstrated genuine repentance as he sought to restore what he had wrongfully taken from others. Grace did not merely overlook his past. It changed his future. The man who had once lived for personal gain became a testimony of God's transforming power.

These accounts remind us that Jesus never ignored sin, yet He never refused the sinner who came to Him in faith. His grace confronted people with the truth while also extending the invitation to forgiveness and restoration. Wherever Jesus went, lives were changed because people encountered both the holiness of God and the mercy of God in the same Savior.

The same invitation remains today. Regardless of a person's background, failures, or regrets, the grace of Jesus Christ continues to call broken people to Himself. His power to restore has not diminished, and His invitation has not changed. The Savior who welcomed the Samaritan woman and Zacchaeus still welcomes all who come to Him in faith.

The grace of Jesus Christ is often misunderstood. Some imagine that grace overlooks sin or excuses wrongdoing. The Scriptures present a very different picture. Grace never minimizes the seriousness of sin. Instead, it provides the opportunity for forgiveness, transformation, and restoration. Jesus consistently upheld the holiness of God while extending mercy to those who humbly turned to Him.

One of the clearest examples is found in the account of the woman who had been caught in adultery. The religious leaders brought her before Jesus, not because they desired justice, but because they hoped to trap Him with a difficult question. According to the Law, her sin deserved judgment. If Jesus ignored the Law, they would accuse Him of rejecting the Scriptures. If He demanded her punishment, they believed His reputation for compassion would be destroyed.

Rather than responding immediately, Jesus stooped down and wrote upon the ground. Then He stood and answered with words that exposed the hearts of everyone present. The crowd had gathered to condemn one sinner while refusing to acknowledge their own guilt before God.

"He that is without sin among you, let him first cast a stone at her."
John 8:7

One by one, the accusers quietly departed until only Jesus and the woman remained. The only person present who possessed the authority to condemn her was also the only One who extended mercy. Yet His mercy did not ignore the reality of her sin.

"Neither do I condemn thee: go, and sin no more."
John 8:11

These words beautifully reveal the balance of God's grace. Jesus did not declare that her actions were acceptable, nor did He dismiss the seriousness of her sin. Instead, He offered forgiveness while calling her to a transformed life. Grace always points people toward holiness. It forgives the sinner without approving the sin.

The life of Simon Peter provides another powerful example of restoring grace. Peter loved Jesus deeply and boldly declared that he would remain faithful even if everyone else abandoned Him. Yet on the night of Jesus' arrest, fear overcame his courage. Three times he denied even knowing the Lord. After the third denial, the rooster crowed just as Jesus had foretold, and Peter realized the weight of what he had done.

His failure could have become the defining moment of his life. Many people would have concluded that someone capable of denying the Savior could never again be trusted with responsibility in God's kingdom. Jesus, however, had not finished writing Peter's story.

After His resurrection, Jesus sought Peter, not to humiliate him, but to restore him. Beside the Sea of Galilee, the Lord gently asked Peter three times whether he loved Him. Each question corresponded to one of Peter's earlier denials, not as a reminder of failure, but as an opportunity for restoration. The conversation ended not with rejection but with a renewed commission to care for God's people.

"So when they had dined, Jesus saith to Simon Peter, Simon, son of Jonas, lovest thou me?... Feed my sheep."
John 21:15–17

Peter's story reminds us that failure does not have the final word when grace is received. The man who once denied Jesus later stood boldly on the Day of Pentecost and proclaimed the gospel with courage and conviction. God's grace restored what fear had nearly destroyed.

Whether the brokenness is caused by personal sin, painful circumstances, or years of regret, the invitation of Jesus Christ remains the same. He does not promise merely to improve our lives. He offers to make us new. His grace reaches beyond our failures, heals what sin has broken, and restores those who place their faith in Him.

Perhaps no account demonstrates the reach of God's grace more clearly than the thief who was crucified beside Jesus. Both men had been condemned for their crimes and were facing certain death. At first, the thieves joined those who mocked the Savior. Yet as the hours passed, something changed in the heart of one of them. While others saw only a dying man upon a cross, he began to recognize the innocence of Jesus and the reality of His kingdom.

Acknowledging his own guilt, he confessed that he deserved the punishment he was receiving. Unlike the religious leaders who trusted in their own righteousness, this man had nothing to offer. He could not undo the wrongs of his past. He could not begin a new life. He could not perform acts of service or demonstrate years of faithful obedience. His only hope rested entirely upon the mercy of the One hanging beside him.

"And he said unto Jesus, Lord, remember me when thou comest into thy kingdom."
Luke 23:42

Jesus answered with words that have comforted countless believers throughout history.

"Verily I say unto thee, To day shalt thou be with me in paradise."
Luke 23:43

The thief brought nothing but a repentant heart and faith in the Savior. He possessed no accomplishments worthy of praise and no opportunity to earn God's favor. Yet he received the promise of eternal life because salvation has always been grounded in the grace of God rather than the merit of man. His account reminds us that no life is beyond the reach of God's mercy while there is opportunity to respond to Christ.

Another remarkable example is found in the life of Saul of Tarsus. Before becoming the Apostle Paul, Saul fiercely opposed the church. He approved of the death of Stephen, imprisoned believers, and sought to destroy the very message he would later proclaim. If anyone appeared beyond hope, it was Saul.

While traveling to Damascus with authority to arrest followers of Jesus Christ, Saul encountered the risen Lord. In a single moment, the persecutor became the one being confronted. The man who believed he was serving God discovered that he had been resisting the very Messiah whom God had sent.

"And he trembling and astonished said, Lord, what wilt thou have me to do?"
Acts 9:6

God's grace completely transformed Saul's life. The persecutor became a preacher. The enemy of the church became one of its greatest missionaries. The man who once attempted to silence the gospel spent the remainder of his life proclaiming Jesus Christ throughout the known world. His testimony stands as undeniable evidence that the grace of God is greater than the darkest past.

These accounts differ in many ways, yet they proclaim the same truth. The Samaritan woman, Zacchaeus, the woman caught in adultery, Peter, the thief on the cross, and Saul all came from different backgrounds and carried different burdens. Some were known for immorality. Others struggled with pride, fear, greed, or violence. Yet every one of them found the same Savior, received the same grace, and experienced the same transforming power of God.

Grace does not discriminate according to age, nationality, education, social status, or past failures. It reaches every person who humbly comes to Jesus Christ in faith. No one is too broken to be restored. No one is too far away to be welcomed. No one has sinned so greatly that the blood of Jesus Christ is unable to forgive those who genuinely turn to Him.

This is the beauty of the gospel. Jesus Christ does not merely improve lives. He makes all things new. His grace restores what sin has broken, gives hope where despair once ruled, and invites every person into a relationship with God. The Savior who called broken people in the first century continues to call broken people today, and His invitation remains unchanged.

"Him that cometh to me I will in no wise cast out."
John 6:37

These words provide lasting assurance for every searching heart. Jesus does not turn away those who sincerely come to Him. His invitation remains open, His grace remains sufficient, and His power to save remains unchanged. No matter how difficult the journey has been or how heavy the burden may feel, the Savior continues to receive all who come to Him in faith.

Grace Still Calls

No One Is Beyond His Reach

The grace of Jesus Christ reaches farther than our failures and extends beyond our deepest regrets. In the next chapter, we will answer one of the most personal questions anyone can ask: Can I really be forgiven?
