Who Do You Call Savior?

Chapter Three

The Problem of Sin

The Problem of Sin

Every solution begins with an honest understanding of the problem. Before a physician can prescribe the proper treatment, a diagnosis must first be made. Before a builder can repair a damaged foundation, the source of the damage must be identified. In much the same way, before we can appreciate the salvation that Jesus Christ offers, we must first understand why salvation is necessary.

The Bible identifies the problem with remarkable clarity.

That problem is sin.

Although the word sin is familiar to many people, it is often misunderstood. Some think only of the most serious crimes or the most visible acts of wickedness. Others define sin according to personal opinion or changing cultural standards. As society changes, ideas about right and wrong often change as well. What one generation accepts, another may reject. Human opinions, however, have never determined what is sinful. God alone establishes the standard of righteousness because He alone is perfectly holy.

The Scriptures teach that sin is anything that falls short of God's perfect character and His revealed will. It includes not only the wrong things we do but also the good that we fail to do. Sin can be found in our actions, our words, our attitudes, our thoughts, and the motives hidden within our hearts. It is far deeper than outward behavior. It is a condition that has affected every part of humanity since the beginning.

Because God is completely holy, He cannot ignore sin or simply overlook it. His holiness demands righteousness, and His justice requires that sin be addressed. This truth may seem difficult at first, yet it reveals something wonderful about the character of God. The Lord is perfectly good. He never compromises His holiness, never changes His standards, and never acts contrary to His own nature. The same God who is rich in mercy is also perfectly just.

This is why understanding sin is essential to understanding the gospel. If we fail to recognize the seriousness of sin, we will never fully appreciate the greatness of God's grace. The cross of Jesus Christ only makes sense when we understand what He came to accomplish. He did not come merely to improve our lives or encourage us to become better people. He came to rescue humanity from the power and penalty of sin.

The Bible explains that sin was not part of God's original creation. When God created the heavens and the earth, everything He made reflected His perfect wisdom and goodness. Humanity was created in His image and enjoyed fellowship with the Creator. There was no shame, no fear, no suffering, and no separation from God. The world that God declared to be very good was free from the corruption that now surrounds us.

That perfect fellowship, however, would not remain unchanged. Very early in the biblical account, humanity was faced with a choice that would alter the course of history forever. The consequences of that decision continue to affect every person who has ever lived.

The opening chapters of Genesis record one of the most significant events in all of human history. God placed Adam and Eve in the Garden of Eden, a place of beauty, abundance, and perfect fellowship with Him. They lacked nothing that was necessary for life or happiness. Every provision had been made by their Creator, and they were free to enjoy His blessings while living in obedience to His command.

Among the many trees that filled the garden stood one tree that carried a specific instruction. God commanded Adam not to eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. His command was clear, simple, and unmistakable. Obedience would demonstrate trust in God's wisdom, while disobedience would bring consequences that God had already revealed.

"And the LORD God commanded the man, saying, Of every tree of the garden thou mayest freely eat: But of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, thou shalt not eat of it: for in the day that thou eatest thereof thou shalt surely die."
Genesis 2:16 and 17

God did not give this command to deprive humanity of joy or freedom. Every command that God gives is rooted in His wisdom, holiness, and love. His desire has always been for His creation to live in fellowship with Him and to experience the blessings that flow from trusting His Word. Obedience was not a burden but an opportunity to demonstrate faith in the goodness of God.

The temptation that followed did not begin with the fruit itself. It began with a question that challenged God's truth. Satan approached Eve by casting doubt upon the very words God had spoken. Rather than openly denying God's existence, he questioned His character and suggested that God was withholding something good from them. The temptation appealed to human desire by offering independence from God's authority.

That same pattern continues today. Sin often begins long before an action is committed. It begins when people question God's Word, trust their own understanding above His wisdom, or believe that fulfillment can be found apart from His will. Every temptation ultimately asks the same question. Will we trust what God has said, or will we trust ourselves instead?

Adam and Eve chose disobedience. In that moment, sin entered the human race, and the fellowship they had enjoyed with God was broken. Shame replaced innocence. Fear replaced confidence. Hiding replaced fellowship. The consequences of that decision extended far beyond the Garden of Eden, reaching every generation that would follow.

The fall of humanity explains why the world is no longer as God originally created it. Suffering, pain, death, broken relationships, and spiritual separation are not evidence that God failed in His purpose. They are the consequences of sin entering a world that had once been perfect. The brokenness we see around us reminds us that creation itself longs for the day when God will make all things new.

Although Adam and Eve were the first to sin, the effects of their disobedience did not end with them. Every person born into this world has inherited a fallen nature that is inclined toward sin. This is why the problem of sin is universal. It is not confined to one nation, one culture, or one period of history. It reaches every human heart and reveals our need for the redemption that only Jesus Christ can provide.

The Bible leaves no doubt that the effects of Adam's disobedience extend to every member of the human family. While each person is responsible for his or her own choices, every person is born into a world that has already been corrupted by sin. From the moment we enter this life, we experience the consequences of living in a fallen creation. We witness sickness, suffering, injustice, broken relationships, and ultimately death. These realities remind us that something is profoundly wrong with the human condition.

The Apostle Paul explained this truth with remarkable clarity in his letter to the Romans. Looking back to the events recorded in Genesis, he showed that sin entered the world through one man and that death followed as its inevitable consequence. What began in the Garden of Eden did not remain there. Its effects spread throughout the entire human race.

"Wherefore, as by one man sin entered into the world, and death by sin; and so death passed upon all men, for that all have sinned."
Romans 5:12

This verse does not teach that we are condemned because of Adam's personal actions alone. Rather, it reveals that the entrance of sin into the world brought corruption to all humanity. Every person eventually confirms that reality by committing sin personally. No one has lived a life of perfect obedience except Jesus Christ. Every heart has wandered from God's perfect standard, and every life bears evidence of humanity's need for redemption.

The universality of sin is one of the most consistent themes throughout Scripture. It is not limited by age, nationality, education, social status, or personal achievement. Kings and servants, prophets and fishermen, scholars and laborers all share the same condition. The outward circumstances of life may differ greatly, but the spiritual need remains exactly the same.

This truth may seem discouraging at first, but it actually prepares us to appreciate the greatness of the gospel. Before a person seeks a cure, he must first recognize the disease. Before someone desires rescue, he must first understand the danger. Likewise, before we can fully appreciate the salvation offered through Jesus Christ, we must honestly acknowledge our condition apart from Him.

Humanity has spent centuries attempting to solve the problem of sin through education, philosophy, government, scientific advancement, and religious systems. While these efforts have produced many benefits for society, none of them has been able to remove the guilt of sin or restore a broken relationship with God. The deepest problem facing mankind has never been a lack of knowledge, wealth, or opportunity. It is the condition of the human heart.

Because the problem is spiritual, the solution must also be spiritual. No amount of personal effort can erase past sins or make a sinner righteous before a perfectly holy God. Good works have value, acts of kindness benefit others, and moral living is honorable, but none of these things can pay the debt that sin has created. Humanity does not merely need improvement. Humanity needs redemption.

It is at this point that the message of the gospel begins to shine with extraordinary hope. The same God who declared the seriousness of sin also provided the only remedy for it. Long before Jesus was born in Bethlehem, God had already promised that a Redeemer would come. From the very beginning, His plan was not simply to expose humanity's failure but to provide the means by which sinners could be reconciled to Himself.

From the moment sin entered the world, God began revealing His plan to redeem humanity. He did not abandon His creation or leave mankind without hope. Even as He pronounced judgment upon sin, He also gave the first promise of a coming Deliverer. Throughout the Old Testament, that promise became clearer with each passing generation. The prophets spoke of a Redeemer who would bear the sins of His people, establish righteousness, and restore what had been lost through the fall.

Every sacrifice offered under the Old Covenant pointed beyond itself to something greater. Lambs were offered upon altars, blood was shed for the temporary covering of sin, and priests ministered continually before God on behalf of the people. Yet these sacrifices could never permanently remove sin. They served as shadows, preparing the way for the One who would accomplish what no animal sacrifice could ever achieve.

The writer of Hebrews explains that the sacrifices of the Law were never intended to be the final solution. They reminded the people of their sin year after year, pointing forward to the perfect sacrifice that God Himself would provide through Jesus Christ.

"For it is not possible that the blood of bulls and of goats should take away sins."
Hebrews 10:4

If sin could have been removed through human effort or religious ceremony, there would have been no need for the coming of Christ. The repeated sacrifices of the Old Testament demonstrated both the seriousness of sin and the inability of mankind to deal with it apart from God's provision. Every offering upon the altar testified that the wages of sin is death and that forgiveness requires the shedding of blood.

This truth reminds us that salvation has always been God's work rather than man's achievement. Humanity did not devise a way to restore fellowship with God. God Himself provided the way. From Genesis to Revelation, the Bible tells one unified story of redemption. It is the story of a holy God reaching toward sinful humanity through His mercy and grace.

When Jesus Christ entered the world, He did not come merely to teach moral principles or inspire people to live better lives. He came to accomplish what no one else could accomplish. As the sinless Son of God, He alone was qualified to bear the penalty of sin on behalf of the entire human race. His perfect life fulfilled the righteousness that God's law required, and His sacrificial death satisfied the justice that our sins deserved.

The seriousness of sin is measured by the greatness of the price that was required to redeem us. The cross was not an unnecessary tragedy, nor was it simply an example of sacrificial love. It was the place where God's justice and God's mercy met. There, Jesus Christ willingly offered Himself so that those who place their faith in Him might receive forgiveness, reconciliation, and the hope of eternal life.

Without understanding the reality of sin, the cross becomes little more than a historical event. When we understand the depth of humanity's need, however, the cross becomes the greatest demonstration of God's love ever revealed. It reminds us that while sin separated us from God, His love refused to leave us separated forever.

"For the wages of sin is death; but the gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord."
Romans 6:23

The Bible never minimizes the seriousness of sin, but neither does it leave us without hope. The same God who revealed our condition also revealed His remedy. Although sin brought death into the world, God's desire has always been to bring life. Although sin separated humanity from its Creator, God made a way for that relationship to be restored through Jesus Christ.

Understanding the problem of sin should never lead us to despair. Instead, it should awaken within us a greater appreciation for the love, mercy, and grace of God. The darker the night becomes, the brighter the light appears. In the same way, the more clearly we understand the seriousness of sin, the more deeply we appreciate the greatness of the salvation that Christ accomplished on the cross.

Every person must eventually answer two questions. The first is whether they will acknowledge the reality of sin. The second is whether they will receive the Savior whom God has provided. The gospel never leaves us without a choice. It calls each of us to recognize our need, believe God's Word, and respond to His invitation.

Throughout this chapter we have seen that sin is not merely a collection of bad choices. It is the condition that separates humanity from a holy God. It affects every person, regardless of background or circumstance, and no amount of personal effort can remove its penalty. Left to ourselves, we stand guilty before God's perfect righteousness.

Yet the story of Scripture does not end with humanity's failure. From the opening chapters of Genesis to the closing pages of Revelation, God continually reveals His desire to redeem those who have been separated from Him. The problem of sin is great, but the grace of God is greater. Where sin brought condemnation, God offers forgiveness. Where sin brought death, God offers life. Where sin created separation, God provides reconciliation through Jesus Christ.

The gospel is not simply the announcement that humanity has a problem. It is the joyful proclamation that God has already provided the answer. Every promise, every prophecy, and every sacrifice pointed toward the day when Jesus Christ would come into the world to accomplish what no one else could accomplish. He would become the perfect sacrifice for sin and open the way for sinners to be reconciled to God.

Hope Beyond Our Failure

God's Love Provides the Answer

The problem of sin reveals humanity's greatest need, but it also prepares us to understand God's greatest gift. In the next chapter, we will discover the depth of God's love and why He willingly provided a Savior for the world.
