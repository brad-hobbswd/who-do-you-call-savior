Who Do You Call Savior?

Chapter Three

The Blood That Speaks Better

The Blood That Speaks Better

Throughout the Bible, one truth is woven into the story of redemption from beginning to end. Humanity could never save itself. Sin created a separation between God and mankind that no amount of human effort could ever overcome. From the moment Adam and Eve sinned in the Garden of Eden, God began revealing His plan to redeem His creation. At the very center of that plan stands Jesus Christ and the blood He willingly shed for the salvation of the world.

The blood of Jesus Christ is the foundation of our redemption.

To many people today, the repeated emphasis on blood throughout the Scriptures may seem unusual or even difficult to understand. Modern readers often associate blood only with physical life or death. The Bible, however, presents blood as something far greater. Throughout both the Old and New Testaments, blood represents life, sacrifice, covenant, cleansing, forgiveness, and redemption. It continually reminds us that sin carries a cost and that reconciliation with God requires a sacrifice that humanity could never provide for itself.

From the earliest chapters of Genesis, God began teaching this truth. After Adam and Eve sinned, they attempted to cover their shame with garments they had made for themselves. Their efforts could not remove their guilt or restore their fellowship with God. Instead, God provided coverings for them, foreshadowing the truth that redemption would always begin with His provision rather than human effort. Even at the dawn of history, God was directing humanity's attention toward a greater sacrifice that would one day come.

As the Scriptures continue, this theme becomes increasingly clear. Abel offered a sacrifice that was accepted by God because it was presented in faith. Noah built an altar after leaving the ark. Abraham demonstrated his willingness to offer Isaac before God provided a ram in his place. During the Passover, the blood of the lamb protected Israel from judgment. Under the Law of Moses, priests continually offered sacrifices on behalf of the people. Each of these events pointed beyond itself to something far greater than the sacrifice that stood before them.

None of those sacrifices possessed the power to permanently remove sin. They served as reminders that sin demanded judgment while also pointing forward to the perfect sacrifice that God Himself would provide. Generation after generation, lambs were offered upon altars throughout Israel, yet every sacrifice quietly proclaimed the same promise. One day, the true Lamb of God would come.

"Behold the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world."
John 1:29

When John the Baptist spoke these words, he introduced Jesus Christ in a way unlike anyone before Him. He did not present Jesus merely as another prophet, teacher, or miracle worker. He declared that Jesus was the Lamb of God. Every person familiar with the Old Testament would have immediately recognized the significance of that title. For centuries, lambs had been sacrificed upon Israel's altars, reminding the people that forgiveness required the shedding of blood. Yet those sacrifices were never intended to be the final answer. They pointed toward the One standing before them.

Jesus Christ came into the world to accomplish what no animal sacrifice could ever accomplish. His blood would not temporarily cover sin until another sacrifice became necessary. His blood would provide complete redemption for all who place their faith in Him. What generations had anticipated through symbols, shadows, and sacrifices was finally fulfilled in the person of Jesus Christ.

The significance of Christ's blood cannot be understood apart from the holiness of God and the seriousness of sin. Throughout Scripture, God reveals Himself as perfectly holy, completely righteous, and altogether without sin. His holiness is not merely one of His many attributes. It defines His very nature. Because He is holy, sin cannot simply be ignored or excused. Divine justice requires that sin be judged.

From the beginning, God declared that sin brings death. When Adam and Eve disobeyed His command in the Garden of Eden, the consequences extended far beyond that single act of rebellion. Sin entered the human race, bringing spiritual separation from God and physical death into a world that had once been declared very good. Every generation since has lived under the effects of that fall.

The sacrificial system established under the Law reminded God's people of this reality. Every sacrifice upon the altar testified that forgiveness was costly. Every animal offered before the Lord demonstrated that sin demanded the shedding of blood. The worshiper who brought a sacrifice could not witness that moment without being reminded that sin carries consequences and that life itself had been given because of transgression.

"For the life of the flesh is in the blood: and I have given it to you upon the altar to make an atonement for your souls: for it is the blood that maketh an atonement for the soul."
Leviticus 17:11

This principle remained consistent throughout the Old Testament. Blood represented life that had been given in the place of another. Yet even these sacrifices were never intended to provide permanent redemption. They served as continual reminders that a greater sacrifice was still needed. Year after year, sacrifices were repeated because they could never completely remove the guilt of sin.

The writer of Hebrews explains that these offerings were only a shadow of the better things to come. They pointed beyond themselves to Jesus Christ, whose sacrifice would accomplish forever what countless sacrifices under the Law could never achieve. The repetition of those offerings revealed both humanity's continuing need and God's promise that one perfect sacrifice would one day satisfy His justice completely.

"For it is not possible that the blood of bulls and of goats should take away sins."
Hebrews 10:4

When Jesus Christ came into the world, He fulfilled everything that those sacrifices anticipated. He lived the sinless life that no other person had ever lived. He perfectly obeyed the will of the Father, fulfilled every requirement of God's law, and remained without blemish or spot. Because He was without sin, He alone was qualified to become the perfect sacrifice for sinners.

Unlike every priest who ministered under the Old Covenant, Jesus did not bring the blood of another sacrifice before God. He offered Himself. His sacrifice was voluntary, complete, and sufficient. No additional offering would ever be required because His blood accomplished what every previous sacrifice could only foreshadow.

The value of Christ's blood is found not merely in the suffering He endured but in the person who shed it. Jesus was not simply another righteous man who died for a noble cause. He was the spotless Lamb of God, the promised Messiah, and the Savior of the world. Because of who He is, His sacrifice possesses infinite worth and eternal effectiveness.

For this reason, the New Testament continually directs believers to place their confidence, not in religious ceremonies or personal achievements, but in the finished work of Jesus Christ. Our hope rests entirely upon what He accomplished through His death, burial, and resurrection. His blood remains the only sufficient payment for sin and the only foundation upon which anyone can stand before a holy God.

The blood of Jesus Christ does far more than remind us of His sacrifice. It accomplishes what no human effort, religious ceremony, or personal righteousness could ever achieve. Through His blood, God provides redemption, forgiveness, cleansing, reconciliation, and the assurance of eternal life. Every blessing of salvation is rooted in the finished work of Jesus Christ upon the cross.

One of the greatest blessings secured through Christ's blood is redemption. In the Scriptures, redemption refers to the price paid to secure the freedom of another. It carries the idea of purchasing someone out of bondage and releasing them from captivity. Humanity was enslaved by sin, unable to free itself from its guilt or its power. Left to ourselves, we had no hope of escaping the penalty that sin demanded.

Jesus Christ willingly paid the price that we could never pay. His blood purchased our freedom, not with earthly wealth or temporary possessions, but with a sacrifice of infinite value. Because of His redeeming work, those who trust in Him are no longer slaves to sin but are invited into a restored relationship with God.

"In whom we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins, according to the riches of his grace."
Ephesians 1:7

Notice that redemption is inseparably connected with forgiveness. God does not merely overlook sin or pretend that it never existed. He forgives because the penalty has already been satisfied through Jesus Christ. His forgiveness is neither incomplete nor uncertain. It is grounded in the perfect sacrifice of His Son and extended according to the immeasurable riches of His grace.

The blood of Christ also brings cleansing. Sin leaves a stain that no amount of good works, personal determination, or religious observance can remove. Humanity often attempts to overcome guilt through self improvement, charitable acts, or outward morality, yet none of these efforts can cleanse the conscience before God. Only the blood of Jesus Christ possesses the power to wash away sin completely.

"But if we walk in the light, as he is in the light, we have fellowship one with another, and the blood of Jesus Christ his Son cleanseth us from all sin."
1 John 1:7

John does not say that Christ's blood cleanses some sins or only the less serious offenses. He declares that the blood of Jesus Christ cleanses from all sin. No life is too broken, no past too dark, and no failure too great for the cleansing power of the Savior's blood. Wherever genuine repentance and faith are found, God's forgiveness is complete.

The blood of Christ also reconciles those who were once separated from God. Sin created a barrier between humanity and its Creator, leaving every person spiritually estranged from the One who gave them life. Through Jesus Christ, that separation has been removed. The hostility created by sin has been overcome, and peace with God is now possible because of the sacrifice offered upon the cross.

"But now in Christ Jesus ye who sometimes were far off are made nigh by the blood of Christ."
Ephesians 2:13

This beautiful promise reminds us that salvation is not merely the cancellation of a debt. It is the restoration of a relationship. Through His blood, Jesus Christ brings those who were far away into the very presence of God. Those who were once strangers become members of His family. Those who were separated by sin are welcomed through grace into fellowship with their heavenly Father.

Every blessing that belongs to the believer rests upon this unshakable foundation. We are redeemed because of His blood. We are forgiven because of His blood. We are cleansed because of His blood. We are reconciled because of His blood. None of these blessings are earned through human merit. They are received because Jesus Christ accomplished everything necessary for our salvation through His perfect sacrifice.

The work accomplished through the blood of Jesus Christ reaches even further than redemption, forgiveness, cleansing, and reconciliation. The writer of Hebrews reminds us that the blood of Christ continues to speak today. Unlike the sacrifices offered under the Old Covenant, His sacrifice never loses its power or its significance. It stands forever as the complete and final provision for humanity's salvation.

Near the close of Hebrews, believers are encouraged to look beyond Mount Sinai, where the Law revealed God's holiness and humanity's guilt, and instead look to Mount Zion, where God's grace has been fully revealed through Jesus Christ. The contrast is intentional. The old covenant revealed mankind's need for redemption, while the new covenant proclaims that redemption has been accomplished through the Savior.

"And to Jesus the mediator of the new covenant, and to the blood of sprinkling, that speaketh better things than that of Abel."
Hebrews 12:24

To understand this remarkable statement, we must return to the opening chapters of Genesis. After Cain murdered his brother Abel, the Lord confronted him concerning his sin.

"And he said, What hast thou done? the voice of thy brother's blood crieth unto me from the ground."
Genesis 4:10

Abel's blood cried out because an innocent life had been taken. It testified that justice had been violated and that guilt demanded accountability. His blood spoke of violence, condemnation, and the tragic consequences of sin. It revealed that humanity's rebellion had reached even greater depths than the fall in the Garden of Eden.

The blood of Jesus Christ speaks a far better message.

Abel's blood cried for justice. Christ's blood proclaims mercy.

Abel's blood testified that sin brings death. Christ's blood declares that eternal life has been made available through His sacrifice. Abel's blood exposed humanity's guilt before a holy God. Christ's blood announces that forgiveness has been secured for all who come to Him in faith. Abel's blood reminded the world of what sin had destroyed. Christ's blood proclaims what God's grace has restored.

The difference could not be greater. One speaks of judgment. The other speaks of redemption. One reveals humanity's failure. The other reveals God's victory. One points to the consequences of sin. The other points to the triumph of the Savior.

Because of the blood of Jesus Christ, believers no longer stand before God clothed in their own righteousness. They stand clothed in the righteousness of Christ. They no longer approach God hoping that their good works will outweigh their failures. They come confidently because the perfect sacrifice has already been offered on their behalf.

"Having therefore, brethren, boldness to enter into the holiest by the blood of Jesus."
Hebrews 10:19

This confidence is not based upon personal merit or spiritual achievement. It rests entirely upon the finished work of Jesus Christ. The blood that He shed upon the cross remains sufficient today just as it was when He first declared, "It is finished." Nothing needs to be added to His sacrifice, and nothing can improve upon what He has already accomplished.

For this reason, the blood of Jesus Christ remains the believer's constant hope. It reminds us that forgiveness is complete, redemption is secure, and God's promises are trustworthy. When our hearts remember our failures, His blood reminds us of His grace. When guilt seeks to condemn us, His blood reminds us that the debt has already been paid. When we question whether God can truly forgive, His blood continues to speak with unwavering certainty of the mercy found through Jesus Christ alone.

From Genesis to Revelation, Scripture tells one story of redemption, and at the center of that story stands the Lamb of God. Every sacrifice anticipated Him. Every prophecy pointed toward Him. Every promise finds its fulfillment in Him. The blood that flowed from Calvary continues to speak today, declaring forgiveness instead of condemnation, hope instead of despair, life instead of death, and peace instead of separation. Truly, it speaks better things than the blood of Abel.

The Voice of Redemption

The Blood Still Speaks

The blood of Jesus Christ has accomplished everything necessary for our redemption. It continues to proclaim God's mercy, forgiveness, and grace to all who come to Him in faith. In the next chapter, we will discover how that grace reaches people from every background, every struggle, and every walk of life through the Savior who still calls the broken to Himself.
