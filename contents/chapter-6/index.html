Who Do You Call Savior?

Chapter Six

More Than a Changed Life

More Than a Changed Life

When a person places his or her faith in Jesus Christ, something far greater takes place than simply deciding to become a better person. The gospel does not merely encourage people to improve their behavior or adopt a new set of moral principles. It proclaims that God performs a supernatural work within the heart. Salvation is not the renovation of an old life. It is the beginning of an entirely new one.

Salvation is more than changing behavior. It is becoming a new creation in Christ.

Many people misunderstand Christianity because they view it as a system of rules designed to produce better conduct. While a genuine relationship with Jesus Christ certainly changes the way a believer lives, outward behavior is not the foundation of salvation. A changed life is the result of salvation, not its cause. The Christian life begins with what God does within us before it is ever seen in what we do.

Throughout history, people have attempted to overcome sin through determination, discipline, education, or personal effort. While these pursuits may improve certain habits or attitudes, they cannot remove sin, reconcile a person to God, or produce spiritual life. Humanity's greatest need has never been self improvement. Our greatest need is spiritual transformation, and that transformation is found only through Jesus Christ.

Jesus explained this truth during His conversation with Nicodemus, a respected religious leader who desired to understand the kingdom of God. Although Nicodemus possessed extensive knowledge of the Scriptures, Jesus revealed that religious knowledge alone was not enough. Something entirely new had to take place.

"Jesus answered and said unto him, Verily, verily, I say unto thee, Except a man be born again, he cannot see the kingdom of God."
John 3:3

These words surprised Nicodemus because he was thinking only in physical terms. Jesus, however, was speaking of a spiritual birth. Just as every person experiences a physical birth into this world, every believer must experience a spiritual birth through faith in Jesus Christ. Salvation is not simply adopting new habits or joining a church. It is receiving new life from God Himself.

This new birth changes everything. God does not merely repair what sin has damaged. He creates something entirely new. The old life, once controlled by sin and separated from God, gives way to a new life characterized by His grace, His presence, and His transforming power. The believer begins a relationship with God that continues throughout eternity.

"Therefore if any man be in Christ, he is a new creature: old things are passed away; behold, all things are become new."
2 Corinthians 5:17

Notice that Paul does not describe believers as improved versions of their former selves. He describes them as new creations. This transformation is not accomplished through human effort but through the saving work of Jesus Christ. The same Savior who forgives sin also gives new life to everyone who trusts in Him.

When a person is born again, the change that takes place is far deeper than what can be seen on the outside. Although friends and family may eventually notice a difference in the believer's speech, attitudes, and actions, the work of salvation begins within the heart. God transforms the inner person long before that transformation becomes evident in daily life.

The prophet Ezekiel foretold this wonderful promise centuries before the birth of Jesus Christ. Speaking on behalf of the Lord, he described the spiritual renewal that God would accomplish in those who belonged to Him. This would not simply be an external reform. It would be the gift of a new heart and a new spirit.

"A new heart also will I give you, and a new spirit will I put within you: and I will take away the stony heart out of your flesh, and I will give you an heart of flesh."
Ezekiel 36:26

Before salvation, the human heart is naturally inclined toward sin. It resists God's authority and seeks its own way. Even when people desire to do what is right, they soon discover the weakness of human effort. Sin cannot be overcome through willpower alone because the problem reaches deeper than outward behavior. Humanity's greatest need is not a stronger resolve but a transformed heart.

When Jesus Christ saves a person, He begins changing the desires that once controlled that life. The believer gradually develops a love for the things of God that did not previously exist. Prayer becomes more than an obligation. The Scriptures become more than words upon a page. Worship becomes more than a weekly activity. The heart begins to delight in the presence of God because His Spirit now dwells within the believer.

This does not mean that every struggle immediately disappears. Christians continue to face temptation, weakness, and spiritual battles throughout their earthly lives. The difference is that they are no longer fighting alone. God has placed His Spirit within them to guide, strengthen, convict, and encourage them as they grow in their relationship with Jesus Christ.

"What? know ye not that your body is the temple of the Holy Ghost which is in you, which ye have of God, and ye are not your own?"
1 Corinthians 6:19

The indwelling presence of the Holy Spirit is one of the greatest blessings of salvation. God does not simply forgive believers and send them on their way. He remains with them. His Spirit teaches them, comforts them, strengthens them, and continually points them toward Jesus Christ. Every step of spiritual growth is made possible because God Himself is at work within His people.

As this transformation continues, the believer begins to think differently. Decisions are increasingly measured by the truth of God's Word rather than by personal preference or worldly opinion. The desire to please Christ gradually becomes greater than the desire to please self. Although spiritual maturity is a lifelong journey, every genuine believer begins moving in a new direction because a new life has truly begun.

The evidence of salvation is therefore not perfection but transformation. Christians still stumble, make mistakes, and require God's continual grace. Yet they are no longer content to remain where they once were. The same Savior who forgives also continues the work of shaping His people into His likeness. Day by day, He is making them more like Himself.

"Being confident of this very thing, that he which hath begun a good work in you will perform it until the day of Jesus Christ."
Philippians 1:6

This promise gives believers tremendous hope. Salvation is not maintained by human strength alone. The God who begins the work of redemption also continues the work of transformation. His grace not only saves, but it also sustains every believer until the day they stand in His presence.

After discovering the joy of salvation, many believers are surprised to find that the struggle with temptation does not immediately disappear. Some begin to wonder whether their salvation is genuine because they still face battles with old habits, wrong desires, and sinful thoughts. They expected the Christian life to be free from conflict, yet they soon discover that following Jesus Christ involves a daily walk of faith.

This struggle should not lead believers to despair. In fact, the very presence of the struggle often reveals that something has changed. Before coming to Christ, many people lived according to the desires of the flesh without giving serious thought to pleasing God. After salvation, however, the Holy Spirit begins producing new desires within the believer. There is now a longing to honor God, obey His Word, and live a life that reflects Jesus Christ. The conflict exists because a new life has truly begun.

The Apostle Paul described this reality in his own life. Although he faithfully served the Lord, he openly acknowledged the battle that continued within him. He delighted in God's law, yet he recognized the weakness of his human nature. His honesty reminds us that spiritual maturity does not mean the absence of struggle. Rather, it means learning to depend more completely upon the grace of God.

"For the flesh lusteth against the Spirit, and the Spirit against the flesh: and these are contrary the one to the other."
Galatians 5:17

The Scriptures describe two opposing influences at work within the believer. The flesh represents the old sinful nature with its selfish desires and sinful tendencies. The Spirit produces a new desire to please God and walk in obedience to His Word. These two are continually at war with one another. The Christian life is not the absence of conflict but learning to yield more fully to the Holy Spirit each day.

This is why spiritual growth takes time. Just as a child does not become a mature adult overnight, a new believer does not instantly reach spiritual maturity. Growth requires daily fellowship with God through prayer, faithful study of His Word, obedience to His commandments, and consistent dependence upon His grace. Every step of growth strengthens the believer's relationship with Christ and deepens the work that God has begun.

Although Christians continue to battle temptation, temptation itself is not sin. Even Jesus Christ was tempted, yet He remained completely without sin. The difference lies in how temptation is answered. Through the power of the Holy Spirit and the truth of God's Word, believers are given strength to resist sin and walk in righteousness.

"There hath no temptation taken you but such as is common to man: but God is faithful, who will not suffer you to be tempted above that ye are able; but will with the temptation also make a way to escape."
1 Corinthians 10:13

This promise reminds us that God never abandons His children in the midst of temptation. He faithfully provides strength, wisdom, and a way of escape. Victory is not found through human determination alone but through continual dependence upon Jesus Christ. The believer learns that each day presents another opportunity to trust the Savior and walk according to His Spirit.

There will be times when believers stumble. There will be moments of weakness, seasons of discouragement, and occasions when confession and repentance are once again needed. Yet these moments do not erase the work that God has begun. Our salvation rests upon the finished work of Jesus Christ, not upon flawless performance. When we fail, we are invited to return to Him with confidence, knowing that His mercy remains available and His grace is sufficient.

The Christian life is therefore not defined by never falling. It is defined by continually looking to Jesus Christ, rising again through His grace, and pressing forward in faithful obedience. The One who saved us continues to strengthen us, guide us, and shape us into His likeness until the day we see Him face to face.

As believers continue walking with Jesus Christ, the transformation that began within the heart gradually becomes visible in everyday life. This change is not manufactured through human effort alone. It is the result of the Holy Spirit working within those who belong to Christ. Just as a healthy tree naturally produces fruit according to its nature, a believer who abides in Christ will begin to display the character of Christ.

Jesus often used simple illustrations from everyday life to teach profound spiritual truths. One of His most memorable examples compared believers to branches connected to a vine. The branch possesses no life of its own apart from the vine. Its ability to bear fruit depends entirely upon remaining connected to its source of life. In the same way, Christians bear spiritual fruit only as they continue to abide in Jesus Christ.

"I am the vine, ye are the branches: He that abideth in me, and I in him, the same bringeth forth much fruit: for without me ye can do nothing."
John 15:5

This truth reminds us that spiritual growth is never accomplished through self reliance. The Christian life is lived through continual dependence upon the Savior. Every victory over temptation, every act of obedience, every expression of love, and every moment of faithfulness finds its source in Jesus Christ. Apart from Him, we possess neither the strength nor the ability to produce lasting spiritual fruit.

The Apostle Paul described the evidence of the Holy Spirit's work by identifying what he called the fruit of the Spirit. Unlike outward religious activities that may be performed merely for appearance, these qualities reflect the inward character that God develops within every growing believer.

"But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, longsuffering, gentleness, goodness, faith, meekness, temperance."
Galatians 5:22–23

These qualities do not appear all at once, nor do they reach maturity overnight. Just as fruit requires time to grow, spiritual character develops as believers continually walk with Christ. The Holy Spirit patiently shapes our attitudes, our thoughts, our speech, and our relationships so that they increasingly reflect the character of Jesus Himself.

This transformation affects every area of life. Love replaces bitterness. Peace overcomes fear. Joy remains even during difficult circumstances. Patience grows where frustration once ruled. Kindness becomes more natural than selfishness. Self control gradually replaces impulsive behavior. These changes are not simply the result of stronger discipline. They are evidence that God is continuing the work He began at salvation.

For this reason, Christianity cannot be measured merely by outward appearance or religious activity. A person may attend church regularly, possess extensive knowledge of the Bible, or participate in many good works while still neglecting the inward transformation that God desires. Jesus repeatedly warned against outward religion that lacked an inward relationship with Him. God is concerned not only with what we do but also with who we are becoming.

The evidence of genuine salvation is therefore not sinless perfection but steady transformation. As believers remain faithful to Jesus Christ, others begin to see His character reflected through their lives. Though the process continues throughout our earthly journey, every step of growth points to the power of the Savior who is faithfully conforming His people to His own image.

"For whom he did foreknow, he also did predestinate to be conformed to the image of his Son."
Romans 8:29

This is God's purpose for every believer. He not only saves us from sin's penalty but also transforms us into the likeness of Jesus Christ. The Christian life is therefore far more than abandoning old habits. It is the lifelong work of God producing the life of Christ within His people until the day they stand complete in His presence.

Salvation is not the end of God's work in the life of a believer. It is the beginning of a lifelong journey with Jesus Christ. The moment a person is born again, God establishes a new relationship that will continue throughout eternity. From that moment forward, He faithfully works within His children, shaping them into the image of His Son and preparing them for the day they will stand in His presence.

This journey is often described in the Scriptures as walking with God. Just as walking requires one step after another, the Christian life is lived one day at a time. Each day presents new opportunities to trust the Lord, obey His Word, grow in faith, and experience His grace. Spiritual maturity is not measured by how long someone has been a Christian but by their willingness to continually follow Jesus Christ.

Growth will not always be easy. There will be seasons of joy and seasons of difficulty. There will be victories that strengthen our faith and trials that teach us greater dependence upon God. Yet through every circumstance, the Lord remains faithful. He never abandons those whom He has redeemed, and He never stops accomplishing His purpose within them.

"And we know that all things work together for good to them that love God, to them who are the called according to his purpose."
Romans 8:28

This promise does not mean that every experience in life is pleasant or easy. Rather, it assures believers that God is sovereign over every circumstance. Even our disappointments, hardships, and seasons of suffering are used by Him to strengthen our faith and deepen our dependence upon Jesus Christ. Nothing enters the believer's life apart from the knowledge and wisdom of our heavenly Father.

As Christians mature, they begin to realize that following Jesus is not simply about avoiding sin. It is about knowing Him more deeply. The greatest desire of the believer becomes fellowship with Christ. Prayer is no longer merely presenting requests. The Scriptures become more than a source of information. Worship becomes more than a weekly gathering. Every part of life becomes an opportunity to glorify the One who has redeemed us.

The Apostle Paul expressed this desire when he wrote of his longing to know Christ more fully. Although Paul had already experienced salvation, he understood that growing in his relationship with the Lord would continue throughout his life. His greatest ambition was not earthly success but becoming more like the Savior he loved.

"That I may know him, and the power of his resurrection, and the fellowship of his sufferings."
Philippians 3:10

This is the goal of every believer. God is not merely producing better behavior. He is forming the character of Jesus Christ within those who belong to Him. Every lesson learned, every trial endured, every act of obedience, and every step of faith becomes part of His work of transformation. Day by day, believers are becoming more like the One who saved them.

One day that work will be complete. The struggles with sin will end. Temptation will cease. Faith will become sight as believers stand in the presence of their Savior forever. Until that glorious day, we continue walking faithfully, trusting the God who began this work and knowing that He will never fail to complete it.

"Beloved, now are we the sons of God, and it doth not yet appear what we shall be: but we know that, when he shall appear, we shall be like him; for we shall see him as he is."
1 John 3:2

Salvation is therefore far more than a changed life. It is a new birth, a new heart, a new identity, and a new relationship with God through Jesus Christ. It begins the moment we place our faith in Him, continues as He transforms us by His grace, and will one day reach its perfect fulfillment in His eternal presence. This is the hope of every believer and the promise of the gospel.
