Who Do You Call Savior?

Chapter Seven

What Does It Mean to Be Saved?

What Does It Mean to Be Saved?

The word saved is one of the most familiar words in the Christian faith, yet it is also one of the most misunderstood. Many people use the term without fully understanding what it means according to the Scriptures. Some believe salvation is simply joining a church. Others think it is becoming a better person, living a moral life, or trying to do more good than bad. Still others hope that sincerity alone will be enough when they stand before God.

The Bible presents salvation as something far greater than human effort. It is God's work of redeeming sinful people through Jesus Christ.

From the beginning of Scripture to the end, the message remains the same. Humanity cannot save itself. Sin separated mankind from God, and no amount of good works, religious activity, personal sacrifice, or moral achievement can bridge that separation. If salvation depended upon human effort, no one could ever stand justified before a holy God.

This is precisely why Jesus Christ came into the world. His mission was not merely to teach people how to live better lives. He came to seek those who were lost, to bear the penalty for sin, and to reconcile humanity to God through His death, burial, and resurrection. Salvation is possible only because of what Jesus accomplished upon the cross.

"For the Son of man is come to seek and to save that which was lost."
Luke 19:10

Before a person can understand salvation, he or she must first understand the need for it. The Bible declares that every person has sinned and fallen short of God's perfect standard. Sin is not merely a collection of wrong actions. It is a condition of the human heart that affects every part of our lives. It separates us from the God who created us and leaves us unable to save ourselves.

God, however, did not leave humanity without hope. Because of His great love, He provided the one sacrifice capable of removing sin forever. Jesus Christ willingly took our place, bearing the judgment that we deserved so that we might receive the forgiveness we could never earn. The gospel is therefore not the story of humanity reaching up to God. It is the story of God reaching down to humanity through His Son.

"For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God."
Ephesians 2:8

This verse summarizes the heart of the gospel. Salvation begins with God's grace. It is received through faith. It is not something we achieve but something we receive because of Jesus Christ. Every believer stands before God on the same foundation, not trusting in personal righteousness but in the finished work of the Savior.

If salvation is the gift of God, then how does a person receive that gift? The Scriptures consistently point to one answer. Salvation is received through faith in Jesus Christ. Faith is far more than acknowledging that Jesus existed or agreeing with certain biblical facts. Genuine faith is a wholehearted trust in who Jesus is and in what He accomplished through His death, burial, and resurrection.

Throughout the New Testament, faith is presented as the response that God calls every person to make. It is the hand that receives the gift of grace. Faith does not earn salvation, nor does it add to the finished work of Christ. Instead, faith rests completely upon the sufficiency of Jesus Christ alone. It turns away from confidence in self and places complete confidence in the Savior.

Many people mistakenly believe that faith is merely positive thinking or wishful hope. Biblical faith is much deeper than optimism. It is confidence that God's Word is true and that His promises can be trusted completely. Saving faith believes that Jesus Christ is exactly who the Scriptures declare Him to be and that His sacrifice is fully sufficient to save every person who comes to Him.

"But as many as received him, to them gave he power to become the sons of God, even to them that believe on his name."
John 1:12

Notice that believing and receiving are closely connected. To believe in Jesus Christ is to receive Him as Savior and Lord. Salvation is not merely accepting information about Christ. It is entering into a relationship with Him through faith. The believer no longer trusts in personal goodness or religious performance but rests entirely upon the mercy and grace of God.

Faith also involves surrender. It recognizes that Jesus Christ has the rightful authority to rule our lives. We do not invite Him to become one part of our lives while continuing to live independently of Him. We come to Him as the risen Lord, submitting ourselves to His will and trusting Him to lead us according to His Word.

The Apostle Paul emphasized this truth when he wrote to the believers in Rome. Salvation is closely connected to believing with the heart and confessing Jesus Christ with sincere faith.

"That if thou shalt confess with thy mouth the Lord Jesus, and shalt believe in thine heart that God hath raised him from the dead, thou shalt be saved."
Romans 10:9

This confession is not merely repeating certain words. It is the outward expression of an inward faith. The mouth declares what the heart has already believed. Genuine faith cannot remain hidden because it transforms the entire person. Those who truly trust Jesus Christ desire to identify with Him openly and to follow Him faithfully.

Faith, however, never stands alone. Wherever genuine faith exists, repentance will also be present. These two truths are often mentioned together throughout the New Testament because they describe two aspects of the same response to the gospel. Faith turns toward Jesus Christ. Repentance turns away from sin. Together they describe the response of a heart that has been awakened by the grace of God.

For this reason, salvation is never presented as merely agreeing with a set of doctrines. It is the beginning of a new relationship with Jesus Christ that changes both the direction and the purpose of a person's life. Those who trust Him discover that His grace not only forgives their sins but also gives them the strength to follow Him each day.

Throughout the New Testament, faith and repentance are closely connected. Although they are distinct, they cannot be separated. Faith turns to Jesus Christ, while repentance turns away from sin. Together they describe the response of a heart that has been convicted by the Holy Spirit and drawn to the Savior.

The word repentance is often misunderstood. Some people define it as nothing more than feeling guilty or expressing sorrow for wrongdoing. While genuine repentance certainly includes godly sorrow, it goes much deeper than emotion. A person may regret the consequences of sin without ever turning away from it. Biblical repentance is a change of heart and mind that results in a changed direction of life.

Repentance begins when we see sin as God sees it. Rather than excusing it, minimizing it, or comparing ourselves with others, we acknowledge that our sin has offended a holy God. We stop defending ourselves and honestly confess our need for His mercy. This humility prepares the heart to receive the grace that God freely offers through Jesus Christ.

"The Lord is not slack concerning his promise, as some men count slackness; but is longsuffering to usward, not willing that any should perish, but that all should come to repentance."
2 Peter 3:9

God's desire is that people turn to Him. His call to repentance is not an invitation to despair but an invitation to hope. He does not expose our sin in order to destroy us. He reveals our need so that we might experience His forgiveness and receive the new life that is found in Jesus Christ.

Jesus Himself began His earthly ministry with a call to repentance. Before He spoke of miracles, before He gathered His disciples, and before He revealed many of His greatest teachings, He proclaimed that people should repent and believe the gospel. His message made it clear that entrance into the kingdom of God requires more than religious activity. It requires a heart that turns toward God in faith.

"The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand: repent ye, and believe the gospel."
Mark 1:15

Repentance is not a promise that we will never struggle with sin again. Rather, it is the decision to turn away from a life ruled by sin and to submit ourselves to the authority of Jesus Christ. The believer no longer desires to remain where he once was. Although growth continues throughout life, the direction has changed. Instead of walking away from God, the believer now walks toward Him.

This is why repentance should never be viewed as a burden. It is one of God's greatest gifts. Through repentance, the sinner leaves behind the emptiness of sin and begins experiencing the freedom, peace, and hope that are found only in Jesus Christ. What God asks us to leave behind can never compare with what He graciously gives in return.

Faith and repentance therefore work together in the experience of salvation. We trust Jesus Christ completely, and we willingly turn from the life that once separated us from God. Both responses are made possible by His grace, and both point us toward the Savior who alone has the power to forgive, restore, and make all things new.

When a person responds to the gospel with faith and repentance, God does far more than forgive past sins. He brings that individual into a new relationship with Himself. The believer is no longer separated from God by sin but is welcomed into His family through Jesus Christ. This is one of the greatest privileges of salvation. We are no longer strangers to God. We become His children.

This new relationship is not established through human effort or religious achievement. It is entirely the work of God's grace. The moment a person trusts in Jesus Christ, God gives new spiritual life. The Scriptures describe this miracle as being born again. Just as physical birth marks the beginning of physical life, the new birth marks the beginning of spiritual life.

"Jesus answered, Verily, verily, I say unto thee, Except a man be born of water and of the Spirit, he cannot enter into the kingdom of God."
John 3:5

The new birth is not simply adopting a new religion or becoming more devoted to spiritual matters. It is a work that only God can accomplish. He gives new life where there was once spiritual death. He opens blind eyes to His truth, softens hardened hearts, and places within believers a new desire to know Him and to walk according to His Word.

Because salvation is God's work, believers can have confidence in His promises. Our assurance does not rest upon changing emotions, perfect performance, or the opinions of others. There will be days when feelings fluctuate and circumstances become difficult, but the promises of God remain unchanged. The believer's confidence rests upon the faithfulness of Jesus Christ, who never fails to keep His Word.

"These things have I written unto you that believe on the name of the Son of God; that ye may know that ye have eternal life."
1 John 5:13

Notice that John says believers may know they have eternal life. Biblical assurance is not built upon wishful thinking or uncertain hope. It is grounded in the promises of God. Those who have placed their faith in Jesus Christ can rest confidently in His finished work because salvation depends upon His faithfulness rather than our own strength.

This assurance should never produce carelessness or spiritual complacency. Instead, it inspires gratitude, humility, and joyful obedience. Those who truly understand the grace of God do not seek opportunities to continue in sin. They desire to honor the One who loved them enough to give His life for their salvation. Love for Christ becomes the motivation for faithful living.

The believer's confidence is therefore not rooted in personal accomplishments but in the unchanging character of God. The One who forgives is also the One who keeps, strengthens, and faithfully leads His people. From the moment of salvation until the day we stand before Him in glory, our hope rests securely in Jesus Christ alone.

As we consider everything the Scriptures teach about salvation, one truth becomes unmistakably clear. Salvation is entirely the work of God accomplished through Jesus Christ. It begins with His grace, is received through faith, is accompanied by repentance, results in the new birth, and produces a life that is continually being transformed by the Holy Spirit. Every part of salvation points us away from ourselves and toward the Savior.

This is why the gospel is called good news. God has done for humanity what humanity could never do for itself. Jesus Christ lived the sinless life that we could not live. He died the death that we deserved because of our sin. He rose again in victory over death and the grave, and He now offers eternal life to all who place their faith in Him. Salvation is not earned through human achievement. It is received through the finished work of Jesus Christ.

"For whosoever shall call upon the name of the Lord shall be saved."
Romans 10:13

This invitation extends to every person. Regardless of one's past, failures, or present circumstances, the grace of God remains available. No one is beyond the reach of His mercy, and no one is excluded from His invitation because of previous sins. The same Savior who called fishermen, tax collectors, religious leaders, and broken sinners continues to call people today.

Responding to that invitation requires more than admiration for Jesus Christ. It requires personal faith. Every individual must decide what they will do with the Savior. No one can believe on behalf of another person. Salvation is deeply personal because each heart must respond to the gospel for itself.

For those who have trusted in Jesus Christ, salvation brings both assurance and hope. We no longer live under the condemnation of sin because Christ has borne our judgment. We no longer fear standing before God because we have been reconciled to Him through the blood of His Son. We no longer walk without purpose because we have become citizens of His kingdom and children of His family.

For those who have not yet placed their faith in Christ, the invitation of the gospel remains open today. God is still calling people to Himself. His grace is still sufficient. His mercy is still available. His promise of salvation has not changed because Jesus Christ is the same yesterday, today, and forever.

"Believe on the Lord Jesus Christ, and thou shalt be saved."
Acts 16:31

The question is no longer whether salvation is available. God has already answered that through the cross and the empty tomb. The question is whether each of us will respond to His invitation with faith. The Savior has done everything necessary to provide salvation. He now calls every person to come to Him and receive the gift of eternal life.

As we continue our journey through this book, we will examine what it means to live out the salvation we have received. The Christian life does not end at conversion. It begins there. Those who have been saved are called to grow in faith, walk in obedience, and become faithful disciples of Jesus Christ. The God who saves also equips His people to live for His glory.

The Gift Has Been Offered

Come to the Savior

Salvation is God's gracious gift through Jesus Christ. The invitation is extended to every heart, and the promise is certain for all who believe. In the next chapter, we will discover how faith continues to shape the life of every believer as we learn what it means to walk daily with Jesus Christ.
