Who Do You Call Savior?

Chapter Five

Can I Really Be Forgiven?

Can I Really Be Forgiven?

Few questions weigh more heavily upon the human heart than the question of forgiveness. Regardless of age, background, or life experience, people carry memories they wish they could erase. Some are haunted by words they wish they had never spoken. Others live with the consequences of decisions they cannot undo. Still others carry years of guilt, convinced that what they have done places them beyond the reach of God's mercy.

Can I really be forgiven?

This question has echoed through countless generations because every human heart understands the reality of guilt. Even those with little knowledge of the Scriptures recognize the difference between right and wrong. When we fail to live according to God's standards, our conscience bears witness that something has been broken. We long for peace, yet guilt continues to remind us of our failures. We seek relief, yet shame often convinces us that forgiveness is impossible.

The good news of the gospel speaks directly to this struggle. Jesus Christ did not come merely to improve people's lives or encourage better behavior. He came to accomplish something humanity could never accomplish on its own. Through His death, burial, and resurrection, He made complete forgiveness available to all who come to Him in faith. The gospel does not ignore guilt. It answers it. It does not pretend that sin never happened. It reveals that Jesus Christ has already paid the penalty for sin through His sacrifice upon the cross.

Many people believe that forgiveness must be earned through enough good works, enough religious activity, or enough time spent trying to make up for the past. The Scriptures teach something entirely different. Forgiveness is not a reward for human effort. It is a gift of God's grace, purchased through the blood of Jesus Christ and offered freely to those who trust in Him.

"In whom we have redemption through his blood, even the forgiveness of sins."
Colossians 1:14

Notice that forgiveness is found in Christ. It is not found in human achievement, personal morality, or religious tradition. It is found in the Savior who willingly gave Himself for the sins of the world. Because Jesus paid the full penalty for sin, those who come to Him no longer need to carry the burden of guilt alone. The debt has been paid. The sacrifice has been accepted. The invitation to receive forgiveness remains open.

This does not mean that every consequence of sin immediately disappears. David was forgiven after his repentance, yet some consequences of his choices remained. Peter was restored after denying Jesus, yet he never forgot the lesson he had learned. Forgiveness does not rewrite history, but it does change our standing before God. Through Jesus Christ, the guilty are declared forgiven, the condemned are offered mercy, and those who were separated from God are welcomed into His family.

The promise of forgiveness is therefore much greater than the removal of guilty feelings. It is the restoration of a relationship that sin had broken. God does not merely erase a record of wrongs. He reconciles sinners to Himself through Jesus Christ, giving them peace where guilt once ruled and hope where condemnation once prevailed.

One of the greatest obstacles to receiving God's forgiveness is misunderstanding the difference between conviction and condemnation. Although these two experiences may feel similar, they have very different purposes and produce very different results. The Scriptures teach that the Holy Spirit convicts people of sin so they will turn to Jesus Christ. Condemnation, however, seeks to leave people believing that forgiveness is impossible and that they are forever beyond God's mercy.

Conviction is an expression of God's love. When the Holy Spirit reveals our sin, He does so to draw us toward repentance and restoration. Conviction does not leave us without hope. Instead, it points us to the Savior who alone has the power to forgive. Like a physician who identifies an illness before providing treatment, God reveals our spiritual condition so that we might receive the healing only He can give.

Condemnation speaks with a very different voice. It continually reminds people of their failures while offering no hope of redemption. It whispers that the past can never be forgiven, that God's grace is for everyone except us, and that our mistakes have permanently separated us from His love. These thoughts do not lead people toward Christ. They drive them away from the very One who came to save them.

The gospel answers condemnation with one of the most encouraging promises found in the New Testament.

"There is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus."
Romans 8:1

This promise does not suggest that believers have never sinned. Rather, it declares that those who are in Christ are no longer under the sentence that sin deserved. Jesus Christ bore that judgment upon the cross. Because He took our place, those who belong to Him no longer stand condemned before God. The penalty has been satisfied through His perfect sacrifice.

Many sincere believers continue to struggle because they confuse the memory of forgiven sin with the reality of present guilt. We may remember our past failures for many years, but remembering them does not mean that God continues to hold them against us. His forgiveness is not partial or temporary. When He forgives, He removes our sins according to His perfect mercy and grace.

"As far as the east is from the west, so far hath he removed our transgressions from us."
Psalm 103:12

Notice the beauty of this promise. The psalmist does not describe a distance that can eventually be measured or reached. East and west never meet. In the same way, God completely removes the sins of those who come to Him in repentance and faith. His forgiveness is not reluctant, incomplete, or uncertain. It is complete because the sacrifice of Jesus Christ is complete.

This does not mean that believers never experience sorrow over past sins. Godly sorrow often produces gratitude, humility, and a deeper appreciation for His mercy. The difference is that sorrow no longer rules the heart. Shame no longer defines our identity. The believer is no longer identified by what he or she once was but by the saving work of Jesus Christ.

The Apostle Paul understood this truth personally. Before his conversion, he had persecuted the church and approved of the death of Stephen. Yet after encountering the risen Christ, Paul did not spend the rest of his life imprisoned by his past. Instead, he devoted himself to proclaiming the grace that had transformed him. His testimony demonstrates that God's forgiveness is greater than humanity's greatest failures.

When Jesus Christ forgives, He does more than remove guilt. He restores fellowship with God, grants peace to the troubled conscience, and gives believers confidence to approach their heavenly Father. The chains of condemnation are broken because the Savior has already paid the price in full.

The Scriptures are filled with men and women who discovered that God's forgiveness is greater than their greatest failures. Their lives remind us that forgiveness is not reserved for those with perfect records. It is offered to those who humbly acknowledge their sin, repent before God, and place their faith in Jesus Christ. Every account points us to the same truth: God's mercy is greater than human guilt.

King David provides one of the clearest examples of genuine repentance. Although David was described as a man after God's own heart, he committed grievous sins involving Bathsheba and Uriah. His actions brought devastating consequences upon himself, his family, and the nation of Israel. For a time, David attempted to conceal his sin, but hidden sin never remains hidden before God.

When the prophet Nathan confronted David, he did not excuse his actions or blame others for his choices. Instead, David humbly confessed his sin before the Lord. His prayer of repentance recorded in Psalm 51 continues to encourage believers because it reveals the heart of someone who genuinely desired restoration with God.

"Have mercy upon me, O God, according to thy lovingkindness: according unto the multitude of thy tender mercies blot out my transgressions."
Psalm 51:1

David's prayer reminds us that forgiveness begins with God's mercy rather than human merit. He appealed neither to his accomplishments as king nor to his years of faithful service. Instead, he depended entirely upon the compassion of God. Although David experienced the consequences of his sin, he also experienced the joy of God's forgiveness and restoration.

Peter's life offers another beautiful testimony of forgiving grace. On the night of Jesus' arrest, Peter denied knowing the Lord three separate times. Only hours earlier he had confidently declared that he would remain faithful even if everyone else abandoned Christ. Yet fear overcame his courage, and his words fulfilled exactly what Jesus had foretold.

After hearing the rooster crow, Peter immediately recognized the seriousness of what he had done. The Scriptures tell us that he went out and wept bitterly. His sorrow was real because his love for Christ was genuine, even though his faith had faltered in a moment of weakness.

Following His resurrection, Jesus did not reject Peter because of his failure. Instead, He lovingly restored him. The disciple who once denied Christ would soon stand before thousands on the Day of Pentecost, boldly proclaiming the gospel with courage and conviction. Peter's failure did not become the end of his story because the grace of Jesus Christ wrote a new chapter.

Perhaps no parable illustrates God's forgiving heart more clearly than the account of the prodigal son. After demanding his inheritance, the younger son left his father's house and wasted everything through reckless living. When famine came, he found himself hungry, alone, and broken. The pleasures that had once seemed so attractive left him empty and desperate.

In that place of desperation, the son came to his senses. He realized that even the servants in his father's house lived better than he did. Determined to confess his sin, he began the journey home, expecting nothing more than the opportunity to serve as a hired servant.

"But when he was yet a great way off, his father saw him, and had compassion, and ran, and fell on his neck, and kissed him."
Luke 15:20

The father's response beautifully reflects the heart of God toward those who genuinely repent. He did not meet his son with rejection or humiliation. He welcomed him with compassion, restored him to the family, and rejoiced because the one who had been lost had returned home. The son's past did not prevent the father's forgiveness because love had been waiting for his return.

These accounts reveal a consistent truth throughout Scripture. Whether the sinner is a king, a fisherman, or a wandering son, God's mercy remains available to those who turn to Him. His forgiveness is not based upon the greatness of our failures but upon the greatness of His grace. No sin is too great for the Savior whose blood was shed for the redemption of the world.

Even after receiving God's forgiveness, many believers continue to wrestle with guilt and shame. They know the Scriptures promise forgiveness, yet memories of past failures continue to surface. Old mistakes seem impossible to forget, and the enemy gladly uses those memories to plant seeds of doubt. As a result, some begin to question whether God has truly forgiven them or whether their past will always define their future.

The Word of God teaches us that forgiveness and feelings are not the same thing. Our emotions often rise and fall with changing circumstances, but God's promises remain constant. We are not forgiven because we always feel forgiven. We are forgiven because Jesus Christ accomplished the work of redemption through His death, burial, and resurrection. The assurance of forgiveness rests upon the faithfulness of God rather than the emotions of man.

Satan has always been called the accuser of God's people. His desire is to keep believers focused on yesterday's failures rather than today's grace. He continually reminds people of sins that God has already forgiven, hoping they will live in fear instead of freedom. Yet the gospel directs our attention away from our past and toward the finished work of Jesus Christ.

"If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins, and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness."
1 John 1:9

Notice that this promise is rooted in God's character. John does not say that God forgives because we deserve it or because we have earned another opportunity. He forgives because He is faithful and just. The penalty for sin has already been satisfied through the sacrifice of Jesus Christ. God's forgiveness rests upon His unchanging faithfulness, not upon our fluctuating emotions.

Shame often tells us that our identity is forever connected to our worst decisions. The gospel tells a different story. Those who belong to Jesus Christ are no longer defined by what they once were. They are defined by who they have become through His saving grace. The sinner becomes forgiven. The guilty become justified. The broken become restored. Those who were once far from God are brought near through the blood of Jesus Christ.

The Apostle Paul understood this transformation personally. He never denied the reality of his past, but neither did he allow his past to prevent him from serving the Lord. Instead of living under continual condemnation, he rejoiced in the grace that had changed his life. His confidence rested not in his own righteousness but in the righteousness of Jesus Christ.

"Therefore if any man be in Christ, he is a new creature: old things are passed away; behold, all things are become new."
2 Corinthians 5:17

This does not mean believers instantly forget every painful memory or never experience moments of regret. Rather, it means those memories no longer possess the authority to define who we are. Our past has been placed beneath the blood of Jesus Christ. The forgiveness He offers is complete, and His grace is sufficient for every believer who continues to trust Him.

Living in God's forgiveness requires faith. Each day we choose whether we will believe the accusations of our past or the promises of God's Word. The enemy points backward toward failure. Jesus Christ continually points forward toward hope, restoration, and a life transformed by His grace.

When God forgives, He does not merely remove our guilt. He restores our fellowship with Himself, gives peace to the troubled heart, and enables us to walk forward with confidence. The chains of shame are broken, not because we have become perfect, but because our perfect Savior has finished the work that only He could accomplish.

As we return to the question that has guided this chapter, the answer becomes unmistakably clear. Can you really be forgiven? The answer is not found in human opinion, personal feelings, or religious tradition. It is found in the unchanging promises of God's Word. The gospel declares that forgiveness is available to every person who comes to Jesus Christ in genuine repentance and faith.

No sin is greater than the grace of God. No failure is beyond the reach of the cross. No life is so broken that Jesus Christ cannot restore it. Throughout this book we have seen that every person stands equally in need of a Savior, that the blood of Jesus Christ speaks forgiveness and redemption, and that His grace reaches people from every background and every walk of life. Those truths now lead us to this wonderful assurance: the forgiveness purchased by Christ is sufficient for every sinner who comes to Him.

This does not mean that sin should ever be treated lightly. Sin separates humanity from God and required nothing less than the sacrifice of His only begotten Son. Forgiveness was costly. It was purchased by the blood of Jesus Christ. Because of that sacrifice, God can remain perfectly holy while extending perfect mercy to those who believe.

When God forgives, He does not place His children on probation, waiting for another opportunity to reject them. He welcomes them into His family through Jesus Christ. The relationship that sin destroyed is restored. The burden of guilt is lifted. The fear of condemnation is removed. The believer is given a new standing before God, not because of personal righteousness, but because of the righteousness of Christ.

"For I will be merciful to their unrighteousness, and their sins and their iniquities will I remember no more."
Hebrews 8:12

What an incredible promise. God does not forgive reluctantly or partially. He completely removes the guilt of those who come to Him through Jesus Christ. When the Scriptures declare that He remembers our sins no more, they do not suggest that God loses knowledge of the past. Rather, they reveal that He no longer holds those forgiven sins against the believer. The debt has been paid. Justice has been satisfied. Mercy has triumphed through the cross of Christ.

Perhaps you have spent years believing that your past has disqualified you from God's love. Perhaps guilt has robbed you of peace, or shame has convinced you that forgiveness is meant for someone else. If so, hear the invitation of the gospel once more. Jesus Christ still receives those who come to Him. His mercy has not been exhausted. His grace has not diminished. His power to forgive has not changed.

The Savior who forgave David, restored Peter, welcomed the prodigal son, extended mercy to the thief on the cross, and transformed Saul of Tarsus continues to forgive today. His invitation remains open because His sacrifice remains sufficient.

"Come unto me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest."
Matthew 11:28

The forgiveness of Jesus Christ does more than erase the record of sin. It gives peace to the troubled conscience, hope to the weary heart, and assurance to those who place their faith in Him. Those who have been forgiven are no longer prisoners of yesterday. They are free to walk in the newness of life that only Jesus Christ can give.

The answer to our question is therefore certain.

Yes. Through Jesus Christ, you really can be forgiven.

Forgiveness Has Been Given

Walk in the Freedom of Christ

The Savior who forgives also transforms. Having received His forgiveness, we are now called to follow Him in faithful obedience. In the next chapter, we will discover what it truly means to become a disciple of Jesus Christ and to walk with Him each day.
